LASIK vs. PRK: Key Differences and How to Choose the Right Procedure

Learn the fundamental differences between LASIK and PRK, including recovery time, candidacy requirements, and which procedure may be right for your vision goals.

Last updated on July 1, 2026

When considering vision correction surgery, two of the most common procedures you'll encounter are LASIK (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis) and PRK (Photorefractive Keratectomy). Both use excimer lasers to reshape the cornea and correct refractive errors, but they differ significantly in technique, recovery, and candidacy.

How LASIK Works

LASIK involves creating a thin corneal flap using a femtosecond laser or microkeratome. The flap is lifted, the underlying corneal tissue is reshaped with an excimer laser, and the flap is repositioned. Because the surface epithelium remains largely intact, recovery is typically faster and more comfortable.

Key characteristics of LASIK:

  • Visual recovery within 24–48 hours for most patients
  • Minimal discomfort during the first day
  • Requires sufficient corneal thickness for flap creation
  • Not ideal for patients with thin corneas or certain corneal irregularities

How PRK Works

PRK removes the outer epithelial layer entirely before reshaping the cornea. The epithelium regenerates naturally over 3–5 days. Because no flap is created, PRK preserves more corneal structural integrity and is often recommended for patients who don't qualify for LASIK.

Key characteristics of PRK:

  • Full visual stabilization may take 2–4 weeks
  • More discomfort during the first 3–5 days as epithelium heals
  • Suitable for patients with thinner corneas
  • No risk of flap-related complications (displacement, striae)

Comparing Outcomes

Long-term visual outcomes between LASIK and PRK are comparable for most patients. Studies show that both procedures achieve similar levels of visual acuity at the 6-month and 12-month marks. The primary differences lie in recovery experience and candidacy, not final visual quality.

Patients with active lifestyles or professions where eye trauma is a risk (military, martial arts, contact sports) may prefer PRK due to the absence of a corneal flap. Conversely, patients seeking the fastest return to work and daily activities often choose LASIK.

How to Decide

The right procedure depends on your corneal thickness, prescription, lifestyle, and surgeon's recommendation. A comprehensive pre-operative examination — including corneal topography, pachymetry, and wavefront analysis — is essential.
